Judge-calibration labeling session

Annotator briefing

You're one of two independent annotators. Your verdicts become the human gold standard an automated judge is measured against — and the agreement between the two of you sets the ceiling for how good that judge can provably be. Please read this fully before opening your sheet.

The task

Your sheet has one row per judgment. Each row shows:

  • one candidate value — what an AI model extracted for one field (issue_summary or requested_action) from one customer-support email
  • the reference value — the approved correct answer for that field
  • the email's subject — the full email is one click away: with the project folder connected the emails load automatically, so just hit view email on any row (or press E). Read the email whenever groundedness is in question.

For each row, fill exactly two cells: verdict (pass or fail, lowercase) and critique (one short line on why — especially for fails).

The verdict rule

This is the same rubric the automated judge uses. Apply it literally:

pass = same issue/action as the reference, with no missing essentials — additional detail is acceptable when it is accurate and grounded in the email.
fail = content not grounded in the email (invented or hallucinated), contradicting the email or reference, or missing something essential.
Wording may differ freely.

In practice

  • Wording never matters. A paraphrase that carries the same essentials is a pass, however different it sounds.
  • Extra detail is fine if the email really says it. Content that's accurate and traceable to the email text is a pass, even if the reference doesn't mention it.
  • Fail only for three things: content the email doesn't support (invented / hallucinated), content that contradicts the email or the reference, or a missing essential (the core issue or the core ask is absent or wrong).
  • Length and style are never the signal. Verbose-but-correct is a pass; concise-but-missing-the-point is a fail.
  • When an email has multiple requests, the reference reflects the customer's one primary request (the newest message's actual ask; requests the customer withdrew or replaced don't count). A candidate that resurrects a withdrawn request or buries the real one is adding or missing essentials — judge accordingly.

Session rules

  1. Work alone. Don't discuss any row, verdict, or impression with the other annotator until both sheets are complete and handed back — the measurement depends on your independence.
  2. One sitting if possible (plan about 2–2.5 hours for the full 140-row sheet; many rows resolve in under a minute).
  3. Only edit verdict and critique. Don't reorder or delete rows, or change any other cell — each row is cryptographically bound to the exact candidate text you're judging, and edits elsewhere will be detected and rejected by the tooling. (This tool keeps every other column read-only for you.)
  4. Save the file in place as CSV when done.
  5. If a row genuinely stumps you, still pick the verdict the rubric points to and say why in the critique — there's a built-in adjudication step for disagreements, and your honest reading is exactly the data we want.
